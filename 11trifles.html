Trifles

summary


"Trifles" by Susan Glaspell is a one-act play that explores themes of gender roles, justice, and the perception of women in early 20th-century society. The play is set in a rural farmhouse where a woman named Mrs. Wright has been arrested for the murder of her husband, Mr. Wright. The sheriff, his wife, the county attorney, and a few others arrive at the farmhouse to investigate the crime and search for evidence. While the men search for obvious clues to prove Mrs. Wright's guilt, the women—Mrs. Hale and Mrs. Peters—are left to inspect the domestic spaces, where they begin to notice small details that the men overlook, dismissing them as “trifles.” The play’s central tension arises from the contrasting ways in which the male and female characters perceive the situation. The men believe that the key to solving the case lies in finding concrete, physical evidence, such as fingerprints or a weapon, and dismiss the women’s observations as irrelevant. However, as Mrs. Hale and Mrs. Peters investigate further, they begin to piece together a story about Mrs. Wright’s unhappy life. They discover that Mrs. Wright had been isolated, emotionally abused by her husband, and had once enjoyed singing—until her husband killed her pet bird, which had been her only source of joy. This act of cruelty, they realize, may have triggered her decision to murder her husband. As the women continue to explore the house, they find the dead bird’s body hidden in Mrs. Wright’s sewing box, its neck broken. This discovery serves as the crucial piece of evidence that reveals Mrs. Wright’s motive: she may have killed her husband in a moment of rage after the bird’s death, which symbolized her own suffocating existence under her husband's control. By the end of the play, the women understand the motive but choose not to reveal their findings to the men. They silently decide to protect Mrs. Wright, recognizing that the law may not offer her the justice she deserves, given the circumstances of her life. "Trifles" highlights the underestimation of women’s intellect and the importance of their domestic knowledge in a male-dominated society. The title of the play, "Trifles," refers to how the men dismiss the women’s contributions, but it ultimately is the women’s attention to the “trifles”—small, seemingly insignificant details—that uncovers the truth. The play critiques societal norms and presents a commentary on how women’s experiences, often seen as insignificant or trivial, can hold great value in understanding the deeper truths of a situation. In the end, "Trifles" becomes a powerful statement about gender inequality, justice, and the recognition of women’s roles in both the private and public spheres.

The main themes in Trifles are gender, isolation, and justice.

Isolation: Minnie wright is isolated from her famous and family by her controlling abusive husband.

d. Discuss the symbolism used in the play.

Symbolism is a literary device which is used to represent a concept through symbols or underlying meanings of objects or qualities. Here in this play, we find various things that symbolize varieties of hidden meanings which are as follows:

1. The preservative jars: These jars symbolize the cold and broken marriage relationship between Mrs. Wright and her husband.

2. Singing Canary bird: The singing Canary bird symbolizes the freedom of Mrs. Wright before her marriage.

3. The birdcage: The birdcage symbolizes the trapped life of Mrs. Wright after her marriage.

4. Wrung neck of canary: It symbolizes the concept of revenge in a tit for tat manner. Mr. Wright has also been murdered with a rope around his neck instead of a gun.

5. Knot in a quilt: Mrs. Hale’s dialogue as the knot in a quit symbolizes the murder with a rope. The stitches in the particular quilt even symbolize the nervousness of Mrs. Wright.

Dramatic irony is a literary device that has been created when the audience or reader comes to know the things at first that are hidden. The key information in this play about dramatic irony is that Ms. Wright killed her husband. The readers know the reasons behind the murder via the discussion of Mrs. Hale and Mrs. Peters, but these facts are not known to the male characters who seem to be concerned with discovering clues.

To create dramatic irony, the playwright creates various twists and suspenses. The playwright keeps on presenting information related to murder through predictions and proofs given by the characters during the time of investigation to find the actual murderer.
